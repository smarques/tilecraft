## Chapter 1 — Tilecraft as a Tool for Participatory Brainstorming

### Playing with Meaning Together

Brainstorming, at its best, is not simply the act of listing ideas — it is a collective act of meaning-making, where participants bring their own perspectives, associations, and intuitions into contact with one another. Yet many conventional brainstorming tools privilege speed over depth, and verbal fluency over other forms of knowing. Tilecraft proposes a different entry point: a playful, embodied, and deliberately slow engagement with language, mediated through the mechanics of a sliding tile puzzle.

### The Vocabulary as a Design Act

At the heart of every Tilecraft session lies a foundational curatorial decision: which words populate the board. The 35 tiles are not arbitrary. They can be selected by the facilitator in advance — chosen to reflect the thematic territory of the session, the vocabulary of a specific domain, or the tensions and questions a group needs to navigate. Alternatively, the word set can itself emerge from a preliminary collective exercise, in which participants nominate terms that feel important, contested, or generative. In either case, the act of choosing the words is already an interpretive act: it frames the conceptual landscape before a single tile is moved.

This makes Tilecraft unusual among facilitation tools. Rather than offering a blank canvas, it offers a curated vocabulary — one that carries implicit assumptions, opens certain conversations, and forecloses others. Participants inevitably notice gaps, surprises, and unexpected adjacencies in the tile set. These reactions are data. They surface assumptions that might otherwise remain invisible, and they invite the group to interrogate the very language they use to think about a topic.

### Sliding and Selecting: Two Registers of Engagement

Tilecraft operates on two distinct layers, and both serve the participatory dynamic in complementary ways.

In **Move mode**, participants physically rearrange the board, working through the constraint of a classic sliding puzzle. This layer introduces productive friction: you cannot simply place a word where you want it. You must navigate around other words, plan several steps ahead, and accept that moving one tile affects the position of others. This mirrors the dynamics of real collaborative work, where ideas do not exist in isolation and every choice has consequences for the surrounding field.

In **Highlight mode**, the game shifts register entirely. Here, participants select connected groups of tiles to compose their own word combinations — phrases, juxtapositions, unexpected collocations. The contiguity rule, which requires all selected tiles to form a physically connected group on the board, adds a spatial and almost poetic constraint: meaning must be built from what is adjacent, from what is near at hand. You cannot reach across the board and cherry-pick unrelated terms; your phrase must cohere as a neighborhood on the grid.

This constraint is not a limitation — it is a generative one. It forces participants to work with proximity, to discover connections they might not have sought deliberately, and to make visible the implicit logic of how ideas cluster.

### Writing the Statement: From Intuition to Articulation

When a participant chooses to save their tile selection, Tilecraft prompts them to write a statement of at least thirty words explaining their chosen word combination. This is perhaps the most consequential moment in the game's participatory arc.

The requirement to write — and to write at length — transforms the act of highlighting tiles from a gesture into an argument. Participants must move from intuition to articulation: from "these words feel connected" to "here is why I believe they belong together, and what this tells us about our topic." The minimum word count is not an arbitrary barrier; it is a gentle insistence on reflection, a nudge against superficial responses. The live word counter makes this threshold visible and achievable, creating a small but real sense of accomplishment when it is crossed.

In a group session, these saved statements become the primary artefacts of the exercise. Each entry on the leaderboard — with its score, its board snapshot, and its written rationale — is a participant's authored contribution to a shared record of how the group is thinking. Viewed together, they map the semantic landscape of the session: which connections were made most often, which words were consistently highlighted, which phrases surprised their authors as much as their readers.

### Playfulness as a Facilitation Strategy

It is worth naming what is perhaps the most counterintuitive aspect of using Tilecraft in a professional or research context: it is, genuinely, a game. There is a score, a shuffle, a solved state, and a leaderboard. This playfulness is not cosmetic. It does specific facilitation work.

The game frame lowers the stakes of participation. When ideas are expressed through tile arrangements and word concatenations rather than direct verbal statements, participants feel freer to take interpretive risks, to express ambivalence, or to articulate positions they are not yet certain of. The puzzle mechanics provide a shared task that occupies the analytic mind, making room for more associative and lateral thinking. And the competitive-yet-collaborative structure of the leaderboard — where everyone's statements are visible and valued — creates a social incentive to engage thoughtfully rather than perfunctorily.

In this sense, Tilecraft belongs to a tradition of participatory design methods that use constraints, games, and material props to make thinking tangible and collective. What it adds to that tradition is a tightly integrated loop between physical arrangement, semantic composition, and written reflection — a loop that produces, at its end, a set of documented, citable, and comparable participant perspectives ready to be brought back into the group conversation.

## Chapter 2 — User Manual

### What is Tilecraft?

Tilecraft is a sliding tile puzzle played on a 6×6 grid of word tiles. Your goal is to rearrange the tiles back to their solved positions — but along the way you can highlight groups of tiles that form interesting word combinations and save them to the leaderboard.

There are two layers to the game:

- **Move mode** — slide tiles around the board to solve the puzzle.
- **Highlight mode** — select connected groups of tiles to compose word phrases and earn bonus points.

### Starting a Game

1. Click **Start** in the control bar.
2. Enter your name when prompted. Your name will appear at the top of the board and on any saved entry.
3. The board is shuffled automatically (300 random valid moves) so the puzzle is always solvable.
4. The **Shuffle**, **Move**, **Highlight**, and **Save** buttons become active once the game has started.

### The Board

The board is a 6×6 grid of 35 word tiles and one empty space. Tiles are color-coded by category:

| Category   | Color      |
|------------|------------|
| Tools      | Beige/tan  |
| Relations  | Gold/brown |
| Activities | Muted red  |
| Materials  | Light tan  |

The score and move count are displayed in the stats bar at the top of the page.

### Move Mode

Move mode is the classic sliding puzzle mechanic.

- Click **Move** in the control bar (or press **M**) to enter Move mode.
- Click any tile that is directly adjacent (up, down, left, or right) to the empty space to slide it into that space.
- Use the **arrow keys** as a shortcut: ↑ moves the tile above the empty space, ↓ the tile below, ← the tile to the left, → the tile to the right.
- Diagonal moves are not allowed.
- Each slide counts as **1 move** and plays a sound effect.
- The puzzle is solved when every tile is back in its original position.

**Tip:** The empty space starts in the bottom-right corner. Work methodically row by row from the top.

### Highlight Mode

Highlight mode lets you select groups of tiles to form interesting word combinations.

- Click **Highlight** in the control bar to enter Highlight mode.
- **Click** a tile to select or deselect it.
- **Click and drag** across tiles to select multiple tiles in one gesture.
- Selected tiles are outlined in green; unselected tiles fade slightly.

**Contiguity rule:** Selected tiles must always form a **connected group** — every selected tile must touch at least one other selected tile horizontally or vertically. If you deselect a tile that was bridging two groups, the orphaned tiles are automatically deselected.

**Why highlight tiles?** Each highlighted tile adds **50 points** to your score. The more tiles you connect into a meaningful phrase, the higher your score when you save.

### Scoring

```
Score = Total moves + (Number of highlighted tiles × 50)
```

Your score is shown live in the top-right corner. Lower moves and more highlighted tiles produce a higher score.

### Saving Your Creation

You can save a snapshot of the board at any time **while at least one tile is highlighted**.

1. Click **Save**.
2. Write a statement about your word combination — **at least 30 words** are required. The word count updates live; the confirm button enables once you reach 30 words.
3. Click **Confirm**.

What happens next:

- Your game session (name, score, moves, statement, and board layout) is submitted to the leaderboard.
- A screenshot of the board is captured and downloaded automatically as a PNG file named `{YourName} - Tilecraft.png`.
- A new game starts after you dismiss the confirmation dialog.

### Keyboard Shortcuts

| Key | Action |
|-----|--------|
| `M` | Switch to Move mode |
| `H` | Switch to Highlight mode |
| `↑` `↓` `←` `→` | Slide the tile above / below / left / right of the empty space (Move mode only) |

### High Scores

Open the menu (hamburger icon, top right) and select **High Scores** to view the leaderboard. Each entry shows:

- Player name
- Score
- Number of moves
- The player's written statement
- A preview of the saved board (click to view or download)

### Reshuffling

Click **Shuffle** to randomize the board again. If you have already made moves or highlighted tiles, a confirmation dialog warns you that all progress will be lost. Confirming resets the move count and clears all selections, then applies a new set of 300 random valid moves.
